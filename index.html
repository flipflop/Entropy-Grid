About This Project

Entropy Grid harvests genuine randomness from the microscopic thermal noise inside your laptop's microphone pre-amplifier, the same chaotic electron movement that makes it physically impossible to predict the next sample value.

Pipeline

  1. Capture Raw audio at 48 kHz via AudioWorklet, with echo cancellation, noise suppression and auto-gain all disabled.
  2. Extract The 2 least-significant bits of each IEEE 754 float32 sample are read directly from its binary memory representation.
  3. Debias Von Neumann debiasing consumes bit pairs: only transitions (01 → 0, 10 → 1) are kept, discarding correlated pairs (00, 11). This removes any DC offset from ADC non-linearity.
  4. Whiten Every 256 debiased bits are hashed with SHA-256, destroying any residual autocorrelation and producing 32 bytes of cryptographically uniform output.
  5. Sample Rejection sampling maps hash bytes to digits 0–9 without modulo bias.

Key Equations

Rejection threshold b < 250 → digit = b mod 10

250 is the largest multiple of 10 that fits in a byte. Each digit has exactly P = 25/250 = 1/10 probability. Bytes ≥ 250 are discarded to eliminate modulo bias.

Z-score (heatmap colour) z = (O − E) / √E

O = observed count for a digit, E = n/10 (expected under uniform H₀). Cells glow blue when under-represented (z < −1.96) and red when over-represented (z > +1.96), both significant at p < 0.05.

Chi-squared goodness-of-fit χ² = Σ (Oᵢ − E)² / E

Summed over all 10 digits with 9 degrees of freedom. A p-value > 0.05 means the distribution is statistically consistent with true uniformity. The p-value is computed via the Wilson-Hilferty cube-root normal approximation to avoid a gamma function lookup table.

Trend Analysis

The chart below the grid runs three independent statistical tests simultaneously, giving a live verdict on whether the numbers being generated are genuinely random. Each test catches a different kind of hidden pattern — serial links between numbers, clustering, and distributional bias — so together they provide a much more complete picture than any single test alone. The longer the grid runs, the more data the tests have to work with and the more reliable their verdict becomes.

Cyan line — Serial Correlation

Asks: does each number influence the next one? If the numbers are truly random, knowing the current number tells you nothing about the next. This line should stay flat and close to zero. If it drifts up or down it means the numbers are subtly linked — like a coin that tends to land the same way twice in a row.

Amber line — Clustering & Alternation

Asks: are the numbers clumping together or ping-ponging back and forth? Truly random numbers should mix freely — sometimes a few high ones in a row, sometimes a few low ones, but no strong pattern. This line should wander loosely around zero. A large spike upward means too much alternating; a large dip means too much clustering.

Blue line — Distribution Fairness

Asks: is every digit (0–9) appearing roughly the same number of times? A fair random source should produce each digit about 10% of the time. This line measures how confident we are that the distribution is fair. It should stay above the dashed threshold line. If it drops below, one or more digits are appearing suspiciously too often or too rarely.

Dashed line — Significance Boundary

This is the boundary between “probably fine” and “something looks off”. The cyan and amber lines should stay between the two dashed boundaries. The blue line should stay above the lower dashed line. Occasional crossings are normal — even a perfect random source will stray outside the boundary roughly 1 in 20 times by chance. Persistent crossings are the warning sign.

What good looks like

All three lines staying within their boundaries, with the chart becoming steadier and more stable as more numbers are generated. The longer it runs, the more trustworthy the verdict. Early on (under ~500 samples) the lines will jump around — that is normal and expected.
